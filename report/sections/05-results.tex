\section{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:results}

All numbers in this section are derived from the six trial CSV files
in \texttt{results/raw/} via \texttt{analysis/plot.py}; the full
reproduction recipe is documented in the Artifact Appendix. Unless
otherwise stated, means are reported with one sample standard
deviation over $n\,=\,3$ independent trials per group.

\subsection{Responsiveness}
\label{subsec:res-response}
Fig.~\ref{fig:response} summarises the three responsiveness metrics.
Relative to the Baseline HPA (CPU), KEDA (queueLength) reacts
$10.4$\,s earlier on average (a $14\%$ reduction), reaches peak
replicas $10$\,s earlier, and drains the backlog $12$\,s earlier.
The standard deviation of the reaction latency is also markedly
smaller for KEDA ($3.4$\,s) than for the Baseline HPA ($8.3$\,s):
the queue-length signal produces a more predictable response across
trials.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../results/figures/fig4-response-bar.png}
  \caption{Responsiveness metrics: time to the first new Ready pod,
  time to peak pods, and time to queue drain. Lower is better; error
  bars show one sample standard deviation.}
  \label{fig:response}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Per-run evidence.}~The three paired timeline figures
archived under \texttt{results/figures/} show the same qualitative
pattern in every matched run: KEDA reaches $5$--$6$ Ready pods first
(lead: $5$--$20$\,s), its queue-depth curve remains below the
Baseline HPA during the drain, and it is the only scaler that triggers
pod removal before the observation ends. The aggregate bars in
Fig.~\ref{fig:response} summarise this run-level pattern compactly.

\subsection{Scale-down Behaviour}
\label{subsec:res-scaledown}
Fig.~\ref{fig:scaledown} presents the most pronounced contrast of the
experiment. Recall from Section~\ref{subsec:strategies} that both
groups share identical \texttt{scaleDown} parameters
(\texttt{stabilizationWindowSeconds}\,=\,60\,s and
\texttt{Percent~50\%/60s}). Despite this parity, the Baseline HPA
failed to remove any pod in all three trials, whereas KEDA began
shrinking the replica set $326.9 \pm 3.1$\,s after the burst ended
and had returned half the fleet by the time the trial concluded.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../results/figures/fig5-scale-down-bar.png}
  \caption{Scale-down behaviour. (a) Elapsed time from burst end to
  the first pod removal. The hatched bar marks the Baseline HPA's
  non-event: scale-down did not occur during the trial because the
  CPU signal remained above the $50\%$ target throughout. (b) Number
  of Ready pods remaining at the end of the trial.}
  \label{fig:scaledown}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Why the Baseline HPA cannot shrink.}~The cause is not the
\texttt{behavior} block but the scaling \emph{signal}. The consumer is
CPU-bound with a $200$\,ms busy-wait per message, so throughout the
drain its CPU usage is pinned at its \texttt{limits.cpu}\,=\,$300$\,m.
Because the HPA computes utilisation as
$m_\mathrm{current}\,=\,\mathrm{usage}/\mathrm{requests.cpu}$ with
\texttt{requests.cpu}\,=\,$100$\,m, the reported utilisation is
$3\times$ the $50\%$ target for the entire drain phase. Eq.~\ref{eq:hpa}
therefore produces $n_\mathrm{desired}$ saturated at
\texttt{maxReplicas}\,=\,$6$ until the queue becomes empty
($\approx\,310$\,s post-burst) and each pod falls idle---a transition
that occurs only after the $\approx\,355$\,s trial window has
closed.

\paragraph{Why KEDA shrinks.}~Under KEDA's queueLength trigger,
$n_\mathrm{desired}\,=\,\lceil Q / T \rceil$ with $T\,=\,100$, so the
desired replica count decreases monotonically as $Q$ does. In every
trial the queue fell below $500$\,messages approximately $270$\,s
after burst end, at which point $n_\mathrm{desired}$ dropped below
$6$; after the mandatory $60$\,s stabilisation window the first pod
was reaped. KEDA thus captures backlog reduction \emph{while it is
in flight}, whereas the Baseline HPA can only observe it \emph{after}
the backlog is gone.

\paragraph{Within-window cost.}~The pod-seconds overshoot remains close
between the two groups ($1\,426 \pm 27$ versus $1\,415 \pm 7$
pod$\cdot$s, a gap below $1\%$) because both scalers hit the same peak
of six pods and most of the trial is spent draining the burst. The
economic advantage of KEDA therefore appears first in the tail: it
starts returning pods earlier, but a longer post-drain observation
window would be needed for that lead to accumulate into a large
overshoot gap.
