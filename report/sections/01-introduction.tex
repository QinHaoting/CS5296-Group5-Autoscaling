\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Elasticity is a defining property of cloud computing~\cite{mell2011nist}:
cloud-hosted applications acquire additional compute capacity when
demand rises and release it when demand falls. In contemporary
asynchronous architectures, that demand typically arrives through
message queues---for example, the order-ingestion pipelines behind
e-commerce flash sales, telemetry fan-in from Internet-of-Things
fleets, or job bursts in machine-learning inference back-ends.
Whenever the backlog grows, the service consuming those messages must
scale out \emph{before} the queue becomes deep enough to breach
service-level objectives.

Kubernetes~\cite{kubernetes2024} has become the de facto container
orchestrator and ships with the \emph{Horizontal Pod Autoscaler}
(HPA)~\cite{kubernetes2024hpa}, which adjusts a Deployment's replica
count so that the observed CPU or memory utilisation approaches an
operator-specified target. CPU, however, is a \emph{lagging} indicator
for queue-backed workloads: by the time it crosses the scaling
threshold, the backlog may already be large, and in resource-capped
pods the reported utilisation can be driven entirely by the
\texttt{limits.cpu}/\texttt{requests.cpu} ratio rather than by the
actual workload. Kubernetes Event-Driven Autoscaling
(KEDA)~\cite{keda2024}, a Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF)
graduated project, addresses this limitation by allowing operators to
scale directly on business metrics such as RabbitMQ queue length.
Internally KEDA materialises a hidden HPA and feeds it the external
metric, so the underlying control loop is identical---only the signal
differs.

\textbf{Research question.}~\emph{Given an identical queue-backed
consumer under a flash-crowd workload, how do Baseline HPA (CPU signal)
and KEDA (queueLength signal) differ along three axes---responsiveness,
user-visible effect, and resource utilisation---once all other
scaling parameters are held equal?}

\textbf{Contributions.}~This paper makes three contributions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item A reproducible benchmarking harness, open-sourced on
        GitHub~\cite{group5artifact2026}, that provisions a K3s cluster
        on a single AWS EC2 \texttt{m5.large} node, deploys two
        identical Spring Boot consumers, and injects a configurable
        burst workload via RabbitMQ.
  \item A controlled comparison of Baseline HPA and KEDA across three
        metric axes, covering both scale-up and scale-down behaviour,
        with every experimental variable fixed except the scaling
        signal.
  \item A quantitative explanation of why the Baseline HPA cannot shrink
        the replica set within the trial window: the CPU signal remains
        pinned at the pod's \texttt{limits.cpu} throughout the drain and
        therefore does not descend until \emph{after} the queue is empty.
\end{itemize}
